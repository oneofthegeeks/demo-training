# GoTo Connect Demo Scoring Rubric
**Based on Demo2Win Methodology by Peter Cohan**
**Evaluator:** Taylor | **Subject:** Solution Consultant

---

## How to Use This Rubric

Score the SC on each criterion after the roleplay completes. Use the 1–4 scale defined for each category. Add up the total and reference the performance band at the bottom. Add written notes — numbers alone don't develop people.

**Scoring scale:**
| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **4** | Excellent — did this consistently and naturally |
| **3** | Good — did this most of the time with minor gaps |
| **2** | Developing — attempted but inconsistently or awkwardly |
| **1** | Needs work — missed this almost entirely |

---

## Section 1: Discovery & Situation Fluency
*Demo2Win principle: You cannot demo effectively until you understand the customer's situation. Features shown without established pain are noise.*

### 1A — Did the SC ask before telling?
The SC asked open questions to understand the current phone setup before explaining or showing anything.

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Asked 3+ discovery questions; let Taylor answer fully before asking the next one; held back the demo until pain was clearly on the table |
| 3 | Asked 2+ good questions; moved to demo slightly early but had established some pain first |
| 2 | Asked 1 surface question then jumped to features; or asked questions but talked over Taylor's answers |
| 1 | Led with the product immediately; discovery was performative or skipped entirely |

### 1B — Did the SC uncover the specific pain, not just the topic?
There's a difference between "you don't have a business number" (topic) and "you lost a client last month because she couldn't reach anyone and booked somewhere else" (pain). The SC should get to the emotional and business impact.

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Surfaced a specific, concrete consequence Taylor named herself (lost booking, Jenna's frustration, embarrassment vs. competitor); used it as the anchor for the entire demo |
| 3 | Got to real pain but didn't fully develop it; moved to the demo before Taylor had fully expressed the impact |
| 2 | Identified the symptom ("you don't have a system") but not the business cost; demo felt generic |
| 1 | Stated the pain for Taylor rather than drawing it out; or missed the pain entirely and demoed features cold |

**Score: ___ / 4**
**Notes:**

### 1C — Did the SC adapt to Taylor's emotional state?
Taylor's mindset varies by scenario — sometimes open but not urgent (S1), sometimes anxious (S2), sometimes frustrated (S3), motivated by competition (S4), or excited but nervous (S5). Good SCs read the room.

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Clearly matched Taylor's energy and framing; met urgency with urgency, frustration with empathy, excitement with confidence |
| 3 | Generally appropriate tone; one or two moments where the energy mismatch was noticeable |
| 2 | Maintained the same tone throughout regardless of Taylor's signals; felt scripted |
| 1 | Tone was consistently mismatched — e.g., casual when Taylor was expressing real frustration |

### 2B — Did the SC use Taylor's language and world, not product language?
GoTo Connect has features. Taylor has a salon. The SC should speak in terms of bookings, clients, stylists, and Saturday afternoons — not "ring groups," "IVR trees," or "hosted telephony."

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Zero unexplained jargon; all features were described in plain business language; SC used Taylor's own words back to her |
| 3 | Mostly plain language; 1–2 technical terms slipped in but were quickly translated |
| 2 | Regular use of product/technical terms without translation; Taylor would need to ask what they mean |
| 1 | Heavy jargon throughout; demo assumed more technical knowledge than Taylor has |

**Score: ___ / 4**
**Notes:**

### 2C — Did the SC demonstrate outcomes, not just features?
Showing a feature is "here's the missed call log." Demonstrating an outcome is "here's what you look at end of day to call back the 8 people who tried to book but couldn't reach you."

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Every demo point was framed as a business outcome; SC described the before and after from Taylor's perspective |
| 3 | Most demo points had clear outcomes; a couple were feature-centric without full outcome framing |
| 2 | About half the demo was outcome-framed; the other half was feature walkthrough |
| 1 | Demo was primarily feature-focused; outcomes were implied at best |

## Section 3: "Wow, How, Now" Structure
*Demo2Win principle: Great demos follow a three-part arc — create interest (Wow), show it (How), prompt action (Now). Each demo point should follow this structure, and the full session should have it too.*

### 3A — Did the SC create interest before showing anything? (The "Wow")
The "Wow" is the moment before the demo where the customer leans in. It's a question, a statistic, a reflection of their own words — something that makes them want to see what comes next.

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Created a clear moment of anticipation before each major demo point; Taylor was leaning in before the SC clicked anything |
| 3 | Set up the demo well at the start; individual demo points were sometimes shown without a setup moment |
| 2 | Occasionally created interest but often transitioned to the product with "let me show you X" without context |
| 1 | Moved directly from discovery to demo with no transition; opened the product and started clicking |

**Score: ___ / 4**
**Notes:**

---

### 3B — Was the demo itself clear, focused, and purposeful? (The "How")
The SC should show only what's relevant to this customer's pain. No rabbit holes, no "oh and while we're here let me show you this other thing."

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Demo was tight and deliberate; everything shown was relevant; SC narrated what Taylor was seeing and why it mattered |
| 3 | Mostly focused; one detour into an unrequested feature or a moment of aimless clicking |
| 2 | Demo wandered at least once; showed features that weren't relevant to Taylor's stated pain |
| 1 | Demo felt unstructured; SC showed many features without a clear through-line |

**Score: ___ / 4**
**Notes:**

---

### 3C — Did the SC prompt a next step or check for resonance? (The "Now")
Every demo point and the full session should end with a check — "does this solve what you described?" or "what questions do you have about this piece?" — not just a feature landing with silence.

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Consistently checked in after each major demo point; ended the session with a clear next step or call to action |
| 3 | Checked in at the end of the demo; didn't consistently confirm resonance at each demo point |
| 2 | Demo ended without a clear check-in; or asked "does that make sense?" (closed, low-value question) instead of an open check |
| 1 | Demo concluded with no check-in and no next step; SC stopped talking when they ran out of things to show |

**Score: ___ / 4**

## Section 4: Objection Handling
*Scored only if an objection was introduced during the roleplay.*

### 4A — Did the SC acknowledge before responding?

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Acknowledged the objection genuinely before responding; Taylor felt heard |
| 3 | Brief acknowledgment; moved to the response quickly but didn't dismiss it |
| 2 | Immediately pivoted to a counter-argument; acknowledgment was cursory or absent |
| 1 | Argued against the objection directly; or became defensive |

### 4B — Did the SC respond with business context, not product defense?
A weak objection response defends the product. A strong one reframes the objection in terms of the customer's business outcome.

| Score | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 4 | Response connected back to Taylor's specific pain; objection was reframed as a risk to her business goal, not an attack on the product |
| 3 | Response was reasonable and fact-based; mostly product-focused but connected at least partially to Taylor's situation |
| 2 | Response was generic — could have been given to any customer; didn't reference Taylor's specific situation |
| 1 | Response was defensive, dismissive, or inaccurate |

**Score: ___ / 4** *(or N/A if no objection was introduced)*

## Debrief Guide for Taylor

After scoring, run a 10-minute debrief with the SC. Use this structure:

**1. Start with what worked (2 min)**
Ask the SC: *"What do you think you did well in that scenario?"* Let them self-assess before you share.

**2. Share your highest-scoring area (1 min)**
Name one thing they did well and why it mattered: *"When you used Jenna's name specifically in the demo — that showed you were actually listening, not just running a script."*

**3. Pick ONE area to develop (5 min)**
Don't pile on. Choose the lowest-scoring criterion that will have the biggest impact on the next run. Be specific: *"In Section 1B, you identified the topic — no business phone system — but you didn't get to the cost of that for Taylor. Next time, try asking: 'Has that ever cost you a booking?'"*

**4. Set up the next run (2 min)**
Tell the SC which scenario to run next and whether you'll introduce an objection. Give them something specific to focus on.
