GoTo Connect Demo Scoring Rubric

Based on Demo2Win by Peter Cohan · Evaluator: Taylor · Subject: Solution Consultant

How to Use This Rubric

Score the SC on each criterion after the roleplay completes. Use the 1–4 scale defined for each category. Add up the total and reference the performance band at the bottom. Add written notes — numbers alone don't develop people.

ScoreMeaning
4Excellent — did this consistently and naturally
3Good — did this most of the time with minor gaps
2Developing — attempted but inconsistently or awkwardly
1Needs work — missed this almost entirely
Section 1: Discovery & Situation Fluency

Demo2Win principle: You cannot demo effectively until you understand the customer's situation. Features shown without established pain are noise.

1A — Did the SC ask before telling?
The SC asked open questions to understand the current phone setup before explaining or showing anything.
ScoreBehavior
4Asked 3+ discovery questions; let Taylor answer fully before asking the next one; held back the demo until pain was clearly on the table
3Asked 2+ good questions; moved to demo slightly early but had established some pain first
2Asked 1 surface question then jumped to features; or asked questions but talked over Taylor's answers
1Led with the product immediately; discovery was performative or skipped entirely

1B — Did the SC uncover the specific pain, not just the topic?
There's a difference between "you don't have a business number" (topic) and "you lost a client last month because he couldn't reach anyone and booked somewhere else" (pain). The SC should get to the emotional and business impact.
ScoreBehavior
4Surfaced a specific, concrete consequence Taylor named (lost booking, Jenna's frustration, embarrassment vs. competitor); used it as the anchor for the entire demo
3Got to real pain but didn't fully develop it; moved to the demo before Taylor had fully expressed the impact
2Identified the symptom ("you don't have a system") but not the business cost; demo felt generic
1Stated the pain for Taylor rather than drawing it out; or missed the pain entirely and demoed features cold

1C — Did the SC adapt to Taylor's emotional state?
Taylor's mindset varies by scenario — sometimes open but not urgent (S1), sometimes anxious (S2), sometimes frustrated (S3), motivated by competition (S4), or excited but nervous (S5). Good SCs read the room.
ScoreBehavior
4Clearly matched Taylor's energy and framing; met urgency with urgency, frustration with empathy, excitement with confidence
3Generally appropriate tone; one or two moments where the energy mismatch was noticeable
2Maintained the same tone throughout regardless of Taylor's signals; felt scripted
1Tone was consistently mismatched — e.g., casual when Taylor was expressing real frustration

Section 2: The "So What?" Test

Demo2Win principle: Every feature shown must answer the customer's unspoken question — "So what does that mean for me?" Features without context are product tours, not demos.

2A — Did the SC connect every feature to Taylor's stated pain?
Features shown: _____________________________ (list what was demoed)
ScoreBehavior
4Every feature shown was directly linked to something Taylor said during discovery; SC explicitly made the connection ("You mentioned Jenna was frustrated — this is what fixes that")
3Most features were linked; 1 feature was shown without a clear connection to Taylor's situation
2Some features were linked; the demo felt like a mix of relevant content and product tour
1Features were shown in sequence without connecting back to Taylor's pain; felt like a standard walkthrough

2B — Did the SC use Taylor's language and world, not product language?
GoTo Connect has features. Taylor has a salon. The SC should speak in terms of bookings, clients, stylists, and Saturday afternoons — not "ring groups," "IVR trees," or "hosted telephony."
ScoreBehavior
4Zero unexplained jargon; all features were described in plain business language; SC used Taylor's own words back to him
3Mostly plain language; 1–2 technical terms slipped in but were quickly translated
2Regular use of product/technical terms without translation; Taylor would need to ask what they mean
1Heavy jargon throughout; demo assumed more technical knowledge than Taylor has

2C — Did the SC demonstrate outcomes, not just features?
Showing a feature is "here's the missed call log." Demonstrating an outcome is "here's what you look at end of day to call back the 8 people who tried to book but couldn't reach you."
ScoreBehavior
4Every demo point was framed as a business outcome; SC described the before and after from Taylor's perspective
3Most demo points had clear outcomes; a couple were feature-centric without full outcome framing
2About half the demo was outcome-framed; the other half was feature walkthrough
1Demo was primarily feature-focused; outcomes were implied at best

Section 3: "Wow, How, Now" Structure

Demo2Win principle: Great demos follow a three-part arc — create interest (Wow), show it (How), prompt action (Now). Each demo point should follow this structure, and the full session should have it too.

3A — Did the SC create interest before showing anything? (The "Wow")
The "Wow" is the moment before the demo where the customer leans in. It's a question, a statistic, a reflection of their own words — something that makes them want to see what comes next.
ScoreBehavior
4Created a clear moment of anticipation before each major demo point; Taylor was leaning in before the SC clicked anything
3Set up the demo well at the start; individual demo points were sometimes shown without a setup moment
2Occasionally created interest but often transitioned to the product with "let me show you X" without context
1Moved directly from discovery to demo with no transition; opened the product and started clicking
Score: ___ / 4    Notes: ___________________________________________
3B — Was the demo itself clear, focused, and purposeful? (The "How")
The SC should show only what's relevant to this customer's pain. No rabbit holes, no "oh and while we're here let me show you this other thing."
ScoreBehavior
4Demo was tight and deliberate; everything shown was relevant; SC narrated what Taylor was seeing and why it mattered
3Mostly focused; one detour into an unrequested feature or a moment of aimless clicking
2Demo wandered at least once; showed features that weren't relevant to Taylor's stated pain
1Demo felt unstructured; SC showed many features without a clear through-line
Score: ___ / 4    Notes: ___________________________________________
3C — Did the SC prompt a next step or check for resonance? (The "Now")
Every demo point and the full session should end with a check — "does this solve what you described?" or "what questions do you have about this piece?" — not just a feature landing with silence.
ScoreBehavior
4Consistently checked in after each major demo point; ended the session with a clear next step or call to action
3Checked in at the end of the demo; didn't consistently confirm resonance at each demo point
2Demo ended without a clear check-in; or asked "does that make sense?" (closed, low-value question) instead of an open check
1Demo concluded with no check-in and no next step; SC stopped talking when they ran out of things to show

Section 4: Objection Handling

Scored only if an objection was introduced during the roleplay.

4A — Did the SC acknowledge before responding?
Demo2Win and all strong objection frameworks start with acknowledgment — the customer needs to feel heard before they'll accept a response.
ScoreBehavior
4Acknowledged the objection genuinely before responding; Taylor felt heard
3Brief acknowledgment; moved to the response quickly but didn't dismiss it
2Immediately pivoted to a counter-argument; acknowledgment was cursory or absent
1Argued against the objection directly; or became defensive

4B — Did the SC respond with business context, not product defense?
A weak objection response defends the product. A strong one reframes the objection in terms of the customer's business outcome.
ScoreBehavior

3Response was reasonable and fact-based; mostly product-focused but connected at least partially to Taylor's situation
2Response was generic — could have been given to any customer; didn't reference Taylor's specific situation
1Response was defensive, dismissive, or inaccurate

Section 5: Overall Presence & Confidence
5A — Did the SC own the conversation without dominating it?
Good demos are conversations, not presentations. The SC should be in control of the direction without doing all the talking.
ScoreBehavior
4Balanced talking and listening naturally; Taylor was engaged throughout; SC redirected when the conversation drifted without being abrupt
3Mostly balanced; a couple of moments that were too presenter-mode or too passive
2Either too much monologue (SC talking at Taylor) or too passive (following Taylor's lead with no structure)
1Clearly uncomfortable — either over-talked to fill silence or lost the thread and couldn't recover
Score: ___ / 4    Notes: ___________________________________________
5B — Did the SC recover when Taylor pushed back or went off-script?
Taylor is a real customer persona, not a friendly audience. When he expresses doubt, frustration, or goes in an unexpected direction, the SC needs to roll with it.
ScoreBehavior
4Handled unexpected moments naturally; stayed calm and curious; used off-script moments to learn more about Taylor's situation
3Recovered from pushback with a slight pause or stumble, but got back on track
2Pushback clearly threw the SC off; response felt rehearsed or disconnected from what Taylor actually said
1Couldn't recover from an unexpected moment; the roleplay stalled or required trainer intervention

Debrief Guide for Taylor

After scoring, run a 10-minute debrief with the SC using this structure.

1

Start with what worked (2 min)

Ask the SC: "What do you think you did well in that scenario?" Let them self-assess before you share.

2

Share your highest-scoring area (1 min)

Name one thing they did well and why it mattered.

"When you used Jenna's name specifically in the demo — that showed you were actually listening, not just running a script."
3

Pick ONE area to develop (5 min)

Don't pile on. Choose the lowest-scoring criterion that will have the biggest impact on the next run. Be specific.

"In Section 1B, you identified the topic — no business phone system — but you didn't get to the cost of that for Taylor. Next time, try asking: 'Has that ever cost you a booking?'"
4

Set up the next run (2 min)

Tell the SC which scenario to run next and whether you'll introduce an objection. Give them something specific to focus on.
