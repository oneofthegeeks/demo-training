# Scenario 3: "Clients Keep Texting Our Personal Numbers"
**Complexity:** Intermediate
**GoTo Connect Features:** Business SMS, shared team inbox, mobile app separation of personal/business

---

## Business Context

Taylor's salon has grown. Clients have gotten comfortable texting stylists directly on their personal phones — to book, to cancel last minute, to ask questions. What started as convenient has become a problem. Jenna (Taylor's lead stylist) gets texts at 10pm and feels like she can never fully unplug. One of the booth renters has started just not responding to client texts during evenings, and Taylor has heard from two clients that they felt ignored. Taylor doesn't have visibility into any of these conversations — and if a client is upset, he's the last to know.

**Taylor's mindset going in:** Frustrated. This is a staff morale issue, a client experience issue, and a management problem all at once. He knows it's a problem but hasn't figured out what to do about it.

---

## Customer Profile (for SC reference)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Taylor |
| **Business** | Shear Bliss Salon |
| **Staff** | 6 (2 full-time stylists, 2 booth renters, 1 part-time receptionist, Taylor) |
| **Current phone setup** | Business number for calls; texting still on personal phones |
| **Tech comfort level** | Moderate — uses apps daily, not technical |
| **Decision maker** | Yes |

---

## Pain Points to Surface

1. Clients texting staff personal numbers directly — no separation of work and personal life
2. Staff burning out and ignoring texts outside business hours
3. Inconsistent client experience depending on which stylist responds (or doesn't)
4. Taylor has zero visibility into client communication happening off-platform
5. Risk to reputation if a client feels ignored

---

## Sample Dialogue

> **Note for SC:** This scenario has emotional weight — staff frustration and client experience are both at stake. Let Taylor vent before moving to solutions. The goal is to make him feel heard before showing him the fix.

---

**SC:** Last time we talked you mentioned texting was becoming a bit of a thing with your team. What's going on there?

**Taylor:** Ugh, it's gotten worse honestly. Jenna — my best stylist — she came to me last week and said she's thinking about setting boundaries with clients because they text her at all hours. Like, 10:30 at night asking if she has anything open on Saturday.

**SC:** And she's responding to those?

**Taylor:** She was. Now she's started leaving them on read when it's late, which I get — but then I had a client call me directly upset because she felt like Jenna was ignoring her. And I didn't even know any of this was happening until the client called me.

**SC:** So you're finding out about client experience problems after the fact, through a complaint.

**Taylor:** Exactly. And the worst part is I can't even go look at what happened. The conversation is on Jenna's personal phone. I have no idea what was said.

**SC:** That's a real business risk — beyond just the inconvenience. If something goes sideways with a client and the conversation is on someone's personal phone, you have no record of it.

**Taylor:** I hadn't even thought about it that way. Yeah.

**SC:** And for someone like Jenna — your best person — if she hits a wall with the work-life stuff, what's the risk?

**Taylor:** *(quietly)* She might leave. Which would be really bad.

**SC:** So what you actually need isn't just a texting solution. You need a way to protect your staff's personal space, give clients a consistent experience, and give yourself visibility into what's happening — all at the same time.

**Taylor:** Yeah. Is that possible?

**SC:** Let me show you.

---

*[SC transitions to demo]*

**SC:** So GoTo Connect has business SMS built in. *(shows SMS setup)* Shear Bliss gets a texting number — could be the same as your main business number, or a separate one. All texts go to that number, not to anyone's personal phone.

**Taylor:** So clients text the salon, not Jenna?

**SC:** Exactly. And here's where it gets good — this is the shared inbox. *(shows team SMS inbox)* Every text that comes into Shear Bliss shows up here. You can see it. Jenna can see it. Your receptionist can see it. Anyone on the team can respond, and the client always sees it as coming from Shear Bliss.

**Taylor:** So the client doesn't even know who's texting back?

**SC:** Right — it just says Shear Bliss. So if Jenna is with a client, your receptionist can pick up that text. Nobody has to be the single point of contact.

**Taylor:** That would change so much. Jenna would stop getting texts at night?

**SC:** Because clients don't have her personal number anymore — they have the salon number. Jenna still has the GoTo app on her phone, *(shows app)* but she controls when she looks at it. It's separate from her personal texts. She can close the app at 6pm and not think about it.

**Taylor:** Can she still see the conversation history with her clients?

**SC:** Yes — all the conversation history lives in the platform, not on her phone. *(shows conversation thread)* So if a client says "I texted about this last week," anyone on the team can pull up the full thread. You're not dependent on one person's personal phone.

**Taylor:** And I can see all of it too?

**SC:** Everything. You're the admin. *(shows admin view)* You see every conversation, every thread, every response. If something goes sideways with a client, you have the full picture.

**Taylor:** *(exhales)* Okay. I need to show this to Jenna.

## Features Demoed in This Scenario

- **Business SMS** — dedicated texting number for the salon
- **Shared team SMS inbox** — all texts visible and actionable by the whole team
- **Mobile app** — clear separation between personal texts and business texts on one device
- **Admin visibility** — owner can see all conversations and history

## Trainer Notes

**What good looks like:**
- SC lets Taylor vent about Jenna before moving toward solutions — empathy before product
- SC reframes the problem as business risk (client experience, staff retention) not just inconvenience
- "Is that possible?" is the buying signal — SC should pause and let that land before jumping to demo
- Demo ends with Taylor thinking about his staff, not the features — that's the right outcome

**Common SC mistakes to watch for:**
- Moving to solutions too fast before Taylor has fully expressed the frustration
- Showing SMS without showing the shared inbox — the inbox is what solves the visibility problem
- Not connecting the demo back to Jenna specifically — this scenario is personal for Taylor

**Optional objections for trainer use:**

**Objection 1 — Existing personal number habits:**
Taylor says: *"But some of my clients have Jenna's number already. They're just going to keep texting her personally."* — Good SC response: acknowledge it's a real transition challenge. Suggest Jenna set a one-time auto-reply from her personal number: *"Hi! Shear Bliss has a new business text line — reach us at [number] for appointments and questions."* Most clients will switch after one redirect. This is a common migration path and typically resolves within a few weeks.

**Objection 2 — Privacy concern about shared inbox:**
Taylor says: *"I'm not sure my stylists will be comfortable with me seeing all their client conversations."* — Good SC response: reframe — the shared inbox only covers business communications through the salon number. Personal texts on their personal phones are completely separate and private. What Taylor sees is the business's conversation with clients, not anything personal. It's the same visibility he'd have if a receptionist was taking messages at a front desk.

**Objection 3 — "Can't clients just email?":**
Taylor says: *"Honestly, couldn't clients just email us instead of texting?"* — Good SC response: validate the logic, then share the reality — open rates for business text messages are consistently over 90%, while email open rates average around 20–30%. For time-sensitive things like appointment changes, clients expect to text. Meeting them where they already are is a competitive advantage.
