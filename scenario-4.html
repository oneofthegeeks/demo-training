Scenario 4 · Intermediate–Advanced

"We Need to Look More Professional"

A competitor is winning on phone experience. Taylor wants the full caller journey.

Intermediate–Advanced
Business Context

A new competitor — Luxe Cuts — opened six blocks away six months ago. Taylor has noticed a shift. A few of his regulars have mentioned they went to Luxe Cuts "just to try it out." One client mentioned offhandedly that Luxe Cuts had a really nice phone experience — "you call and it's like calling a real company, it tells you what to press and everything." Taylor called Luxe Cuts himself just to hear it. He was embarrassed to realize how much more professional they sounded compared to Shear Bliss. He knows his stylists are better — but the first impression over the phone isn't reflecting that.

Taylor's mindset going in: Pride is at stake. He wants the salon to feel established and premium. He's motivated — this isn't theoretical, it's a competitive response.

Customer Profile
NameTaylor
BusinessShear Bliss Salon
Staff6 — 2 full-time stylists, 2 booth renters, 1 part-time receptionist, Taylor

Tech comfort levelModerate — uses apps daily, not technical
Decision makerYes
Pain Points to Surface
Trainer — Surface these through questions, not statements
  1. Callers hear nothing professional when they reach Shear Bliss — no greeting, no options, just ringing or generic voicemail
  2. No way to direct callers to the right person or purpose (booking vs. questions)
  3. After-hours callers get no experience at all — just rings out
  4. The phone experience doesn't match the quality of the actual salon
  5. Competitor is using this as a differentiator
Sample Dialogue
Taylor already knows he has a problem — he's motivated by competition. Walk him through a caller's journey end-to-end.
SC
You mentioned on our last call that you'd been thinking about how the salon sounds on the phone. What's driving that?
Taylor
Honestly? I called the new place down the street. Luxe Cuts. And their phone system is really impressive — you call and this professional voice comes on, tells you to press 1 for appointments, press 2 for something else. It sounds really polished.
SC
And then you thought about what someone hears when they call Shear Bliss.
Taylor
(laughs a little) Yeah. It just rings and then goes to my voicemail. Which still says "Hey, you've reached Taylor." I've been meaning to fix that forever.
SC
When a client calls Shear Bliss after hours — say, 8pm on a Sunday — what happens?
Taylor
It just rings. And rings. Until it times out I guess. There's no message, nothing.
SC
So someone who wants to book with you on a Sunday night — which is probably when a lot of people have time to think about their week — gets nothing.
Taylor
When you say it like that it sounds really bad.
SC
It's a fixable problem. Can I walk you through what a caller's full experience would look like if we set this up properly? Start to finish?
Taylor
Please.
SC transitions to demo — walk through the caller journey step by step
SC
So here's the caller journey we're going to build for Shear Bliss. Someone calls your number. Instead of just ringing… (plays example greeting) …they hear something like: "Thank you for calling Shear Bliss Salon. For appointments, press 1. For hours and services, press 2. To speak with someone directly, press 0." That's your auto-attendant. (shows IVR builder in GoTo Connect)
Taylor
Can I record that myself, or does it have to be a robot voice?
SC
You can record it yourself, have a team member do it, or use a professional text-to-speech voice. Most salons like yours record it themselves — it's warmer. (shows recording option)
Taylor
I'd want it to sound like us, not like a bank.
SC
Exactly right. Now — if they press 1 for appointments, where do you want that to go?
Taylor
Probably to whoever's available? Like my receptionist on weekends, or me during the week.
SC
Perfect. We set up a ring group — (shows ring group setup) — press 1 rings your receptionist first, then if she doesn't answer it rolls to you, then to Jenna. Whoever picks up first handles the booking. The caller never knows they're being transferred through a queue.
Taylor
And if nobody answers?
SC
It goes to a voicemail with a specific booking message. "You've reached Shear Bliss appointments. Leave your name, number, and preferred time and we'll call you back within an hour." (shows voicemail configuration) You set the message, you set the expectation.
Taylor
I love that. What about after hours?
SC
This is the business hours setting. (shows hours configuration) You tell the system: Monday through Saturday, 9am to 7pm, these are our hours. Outside of that, calls hit a different path. (shows after-hours routing) After hours, the auto-attendant says: "Thanks for calling Shear Bliss. We're currently closed. Our hours are Monday through Saturday, 9am to 7pm. You can text us at this number anytime, or leave a voicemail and we'll call you back first thing tomorrow."
Taylor
Oh that's so much better than just ringing forever.
SC
And one more thing — when people are on hold, waiting in the queue. (shows hold music setting) Right now there's silence. You can set custom hold music, or even a message that plays while they wait: "Thanks for holding with Shear Bliss. We'll be right with you. Ask us about our new color treatments this season."
Taylor
(laughing) Can it actually say that?
SC
You write it, it plays on repeat while they hold. It's subtle marketing to people who are already calling you.
Taylor
Okay I am sold on this. This is exactly what I wanted.
Features Demoed
  • Auto-attendant / IVR — professional greeting with press-to-route options
  • Ring groups — calls route through multiple staff in sequence
  • Custom voicemail per department — specific messages for booking, general inquiries
  • Business hours configuration — separate routing for open vs. closed hours
  • After-hours greeting and routing — no more calls ringing into nothing
  • Custom hold music / messaging — on-brand experience while clients wait
Trainer Notes

What Good Looks Like

  • SC walks through the full caller journey — from first ring to resolution — rather than showing features in isolation
  • SC asks Taylor what he wants at each decision point (ring group order, voicemail message) — making the demo feel like a configuration session, not a presentation
  • The hold music moment is a delight — let Taylor laugh and react; that's a memorable selling moment
  • SC ends by positioning the phone experience as brand expression, not just phone functionality

Common SC Mistakes to Watch For

  • Showing the IVR builder without explaining what a caller actually hears — always demo from the caller's perspective
  • Configuring everything without asking Taylor what he wants — the demo should feel collaborative
  • Not showing after-hours routing — that's often the most impactful piece for small businesses

Optional Objections for Trainer Use

Objection 1 Setup complexity

Taylor says: "This seems complicated to set up. I'm not technical. Is this something I'd have to do myself?"

Good SC response: Walk through the onboarding process — GoTo Connect includes a dedicated onboarding specialist who sets up the auto-attendant, ring groups, and greetings with Taylor in a single session. After that, day-to-day changes (updating hours, swapping hold music, adding a staff member) are done through the admin portal and take minutes. Taylor doesn't need to understand how it works — he just needs to know how to change it.

Objection 2 "I don't want to record my voice"

Taylor says: "I'm not comfortable recording my own voice for the greeting. I'll sound unprofessional."

Good SC response: He has options — GoTo Connect has a text-to-speech option where he types the greeting and a natural-sounding voice reads it. Many businesses use this. Alternatively, he can hire a voice actor on a platform like Voices.com for under $50 for a custom recording. Either way, the recording itself isn't a barrier.

Objection 3 "Is this going to confuse my regulars?"

Taylor says: "I'm worried that if I change how the phone works, my regular clients will get confused and annoyed."

Good SC response: The new experience is objectively simpler for callers — they hear a clear greeting, press the right option, and reach the right person faster than before. The clients most likely to be confused are the ones who currently have Taylor's personal cell number. A brief heads-up text or in-salon note ("We have a new phone system — call us at [number]") handles the transition cleanly.
